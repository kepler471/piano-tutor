❯ what is the theory behind piano scales, like for example why certain notes    
  sound off when played together or in arpeggio, until they are played in a     
  particular scale and then they sound fine together                            

⏺ Great question — this is really the foundation of Western music theory, and it
  comes down to physics first, then pattern and context. Let me walk through
  it.

  The physics: frequency ratios

  Every note is a vibration at some frequency (A4 = 440 Hz in your app's
  settings). When two notes sound "good" together, it's usually because their
  frequencies form a simple ratio:

  - Octave (C to C): 2:1 — so similar our brains hear them as "the same note"
  - Perfect fifth (C to G): 3:2 — the most consonant interval after the octave
  - Perfect fourth (C to F): 4:3
  - Major third (C to E): ~5:4

  Simple ratios sound smooth because the sound waves line up regularly — their
  peaks coincide often. When the ratio is complex (like a minor second, C to C♯,
  roughly 16:15), the waves almost-but-never line up, producing a rough
  "beating" sensation your ear reads as clashing.

  There's a deeper reason too: any single piano note isn't one frequency but a
  stack of overtones (the harmonic series — 2×, 3×, 4×, 5× the fundamental).
  When you play C and G together, many of their overtones literally coincide.
  Play C and C♯ together and their overtone stacks grind against each other all
  the way up.

  So why 12 notes, and why pick 7 of them?

  The piano divides the octave into 12 equal semitones. Out of those 12, a scale
  picks a subset (usually 7) chosen so that the notes form lots of those nice
  simple ratios with each other. The major scale's pattern of steps — whole,
  whole, half, whole, whole, whole, half — isn't arbitrary; it's essentially the
  pattern you get by stacking perfect fifths (C→G→D→A→E→B→F) and folding them
  into one octave. It maximizes consonant relationships within the set.

  That's why notes inside the scale tend to sound fine in any combination or
  arpeggio: you've pre-filtered the 12 notes down to a club where most pairs are
  on friendly ratio terms.

  The part that isn't physics: context and expectation

  Here's the subtler half of your question. A note isn't dissonant in isolation
  — F♯ is a lovely note. It sounds "off" relative to a context. When you play in
  C major for a few bars, your brain builds a model: it establishes C as the
  tonal center (the "home" note) and learns which 7 pitches belong. Then:

  - An in-scale note confirms the pattern → sounds "right"
  - An out-of-scale note violates it → sounds "wrong," even though the same
  physical interval might be fine in another key

  This is why the same two notes can sound clashing in one piece and perfectly
  fine in another. F♯ against a C major context sounds alien; the identical F♯
  in G major is the note that defines the key. Your ear judges notes by the
  company they keep.

  There's also tension and resolution within a scale. Even in-scale notes aren't
  equal: the 7th degree (B in C major) feels unstable and "wants" to rise to C;
  the 4th often wants to fall to the 3rd. Music works by creating that tension
  and releasing it — a scale is really a little gravity system with the tonic at
  the bottom.

  Why arpeggios sound so smooth

  An arpeggio is a chord played one note at a time — and a chord (like C–E–G) is
  built by taking every other note of the scale, which lands you exactly on the
  simplest ratios available: root, major third (5:4), fifth (3:2). So an
  arpeggio is basically a tour of the most consonant intervals in the key. Play
  a "wrong" note in the middle and you've stuck a complex ratio into a sequence
  of simple ones — it jumps out immediately.

  One fun footnote

  Modern pianos actually cheat: equal temperament makes every semitone identical
  so you can play in all 12 keys, at the cost of every interval except the
  octave being slightly off its pure ratio (the fifth is 3:2-ish, not exactly).
  Your ear tolerates the small error. Before this compromise (~18th century),
  keyboards were tuned so some keys sounded gorgeous and others were unusably
  sour — which is partly why old composers described different keys as having
  different "characters."

  Since you're working through scales in the app: this is why practicing them
  isn't just finger training. You're teaching your ear each key's "gravity" —
  which notes belong, which pull toward home — so eventually you hear a wrong
  note before you can name why it's wrong.

❯ So what brings about this pattern of whole, whole, half, whole, whole, whole, 
  half                                                                          

⏺ This is one of my favorite bits of theory, because the pattern looks arbitrary
  until you see it fall out of a single idea: stacking the most consonant 
  interval you have and folding the results into one octave.

  Building a scale from fifths

  Start from the physics we covered: after the octave (2:1), the perfect fifth
  (3:2) is the most consonant interval — the one your ear most strongly hears as
  "related." So a natural way to generate a family of related notes is: take a
  starting note, and keep going up by fifths.

  Start at F and stack six fifths:

  F → C → G → D → A → E → B

  Seven notes. Now, notes an octave apart count as "the same" (that 2:1 ratio is
  why), so drop them all into a single octave and sort by pitch:

  C, D, E, F, G, A, B — the C major scale.

  Measure the gaps between neighbors and you get exactly whole, whole, half, 
  whole, whole, whole, half. The pattern isn't designed; it's the shape that
  seven fifth-related notes make when you compress them into one octave. This
  construction is ancient — it's essentially Pythagorean tuning, worked out from
  string lengths over two thousand years ago.

  Why the gaps come out uneven

  You might ask: why doesn't stacking equal intervals (fifths) give equal gaps?
  Because a fifth is 7 semitones, and 7 doesn't divide evenly into the
  12-semitone octave. Each new fifth lands you 7 steps around a 12-hour clock
  face: 7, 2, 9, 4, 11, 6... you keep landing in the biggest remaining gap,
  splitting it. Stop after 7 notes and you're left with gaps of two sizes — five
  whole steps and two half steps, spread as evenly as they can possibly be.
  Mathematicians literally call the diatonic scale a maximally even distribution
  of 7 points among 12.

  And that near-evenness-but-not-quite is the magic. If the gaps were perfectly
  equal (like the whole-tone scale: W-W-W-W-W-W), every note would look
  identical to every other — no landmarks, no "home," famously floaty and
  directionless (Debussy used it for exactly that dreamlike effect). The two
  half steps break the symmetry. They sit in a unique arrangement, so each of
  the 7 scale degrees has a different pattern of neighbors — which is what lets
  your ear tell where it is in the key and gives each degree its distinct pull.
  The half step from B up to C is precisely why B "leans" so hard into the
  tonic.

  Why 12 notes in the first place

  Keep stacking fifths past 7 and something remarkable happens at 12: the
  twelfth fifth lands you almost exactly back on your starting note (7 octaves
  up). Not exactly — it overshoots by about a quarter of a semitone, a famous
  discrepancy called the Pythagorean comma — but close enough that if you fudge
  each fifth a tiny bit (that's equal temperament again), the circle closes
  perfectly. That's the circle of fifths, and it's why 12 is the chromatic
  universe: it's the smallest stack of fifths that comes back home.

  So the whole structure is one idea applied twice:
  - Stack fifths until the circle closes → 12 chromatic notes
  - Take a 7-note contiguous arc of that circle → the major scale, and its
  W-W-H-W-W-W-H shape

  A satisfying consequence

  This also explains key signatures. Since C major is the arc F–C–G–D–A–E–B on
  the circle, sliding the arc one position clockwise (start from C instead of F)
  swaps out exactly one note: F becomes F♯ — and you have G major, one sharp.
  Every step around the circle changes exactly one accidental. Neighboring keys
  share 6 of 7 notes, which is why modulating to the dominant feels like a
  gentle lean rather than a jump — and why the circle of fifths poster hangs in
  every piano teacher's studio.

  One last connection: the minor scale and the church modes are the same seven
  notes with a different choice of "home." Start the C major set on A and you
  get A minor — same arc of fifths, different center of gravity. The W-W-H
  pattern rotates, and each rotation has its own emotional color. All of it from
  stacking one interval.
